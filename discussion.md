This section discusses the rationale and interpretation of the proposed LdaBoost method, with particular attention to its learning mechanism and its relationship to the gradient boosting framework.
Firstly, the proposed approach builds upon the general principles of gradient boosting. In classical GBM for categorical targets, subsequent learners are not trained directly on the original class labels, but on pseudo-residuals, i.e., the negative gradients of the loss function with respect to the current model {friedman2001gbm}. These quantities are continuous and differ substantially from the original labels, yet they provide the supervision signal that drives the iterative refinement of the model.
In our method, rather than applying LDA directly to these continuous residuals, we use the gradient information to construct pseudo-labels. Specifically, after computing the residuals, each observation is reassigned to the class corresponding to the largest positive gradient component. 
This step can be interpreted as a discretization of the functional gradient, identifying, for each observation, the direction in which the model most needs to adjust its prediction. LDA is then applied to these dynamically relabeled data, thereby preserving its natural formulation as a method for supervised dimensionality reduction with categorical labels. In other words, the pseudo-label identifies the class toward which the model “should move” in order to correct its current error.
This procedure introduces a form of dynamic supervision, in which the labels are no longer fixed but evolve across iterations, reflecting the current state of the model. As a consequence, LDA is not used to separate the original classes, but rather to identify, at each iteration, the directions in the feature space that best discriminate among the required corrections. In this sense, the resulting discriminant projection is locally adapted to the model’s errors and allows the learning process to focus on the most challenging regions of the problem.
From this perspective, the relabeling step can be viewed in connection with cost-sensitive and reweighted classification frameworks, where the learning problem is modified to emphasize 
difficult or misclassified observations \citep{elkan2001cost, zadrozny2003cost}, as well as with hard assignment strategies derived from soft signals, as in expectation–maximization (EM) algorithms \citep{dempster1977em }. In this end, LDA plays a dynamic role within this framework: at each iteration, it identifies a discriminant subspace that emphasizes the directions where the model is currently making the largest errors, as indicated by the gradient signal.
Second, in the binary classification setting, Fisher Discriminant Analysis yields a single discriminant component, and it may therefore appear somewhat surprising that LdaBoost performs competitively at all given the resulting one-dimensional representation. However, this component is obtained through a supervised procedure explicitly designed to maximize class separation, and therefore represents a highly informative projection of the original feature space. Rather than constituting a generic dimensionality reduction, it can be interpreted as a targeted feature extraction step that concentrates the relevant signal for the prediction task into a single direction.
Even when operating on a one-dimensional representation, Gradient Boosting Machines with decision trees retain considerable modeling flexibility. Decision trees can introduce multiple splits along the projected axis, enabling the approximation of complex and nonlinear relationships through combinations of threshold-based rules. As a result, the restriction to a single feature does not necessarily imply a substantial loss in the expressive power of the overall model.
From a statistical perspective, the binary case can also be viewed as a setting in which the method trades representational richness for noise reduction and stability. By projecting the data onto the most discriminative direction, the approach may reduce variance and improve generalization, particularly in high-dimensional or noisy scenarios. This perspective helps explain why competitive empirical performance can still be observed despite the apparent constraint. At the same time, the advantages of the proposed approach become more pronounced in the multiclass setting, where multiple discriminant components provide a richer representation for the subsequent boosting stage.
Third, despite the strong predictive accuracy of LDA-based methods, an important finding is that GBM (using cross-validated metrics) outperforms the other approaches on the largest and most feature-rich HAR dataset. A possible explanation is that, in this setting, the supervised dimensionality reduction induced by LDA may compress the original information too aggressively. In particular, LDA produces at most C−1 discriminant components, thereby representing the data through a small number of global linear directions; this can be limiting when the discriminative signal is distributed across many variables or depends on nonlinear relationships among features \citep{hastie2009elements}. By contrast, GBM can naturally capture threshold effects, feature interactions, and complex nonlinear structures through ensembles of decision trees, making it particularly effective in high-dimensional settings with heterogeneous features \citep{friedman2001gbm}. This suggests that, for rich datasets such as HAR, preserving the original feature space may offer a substantial advantage.
Moreover, the literature highlights several well-known limitations of LDA/FDA: performance may degrade when the Gaussian class-conditional assumption and the homogeneity of covariance matrices are violated \citep{hastie2009elements}; when classes exhibit multimodal structure that cannot be captured by a single discriminant direction \citep{sugiyama2007lda}; and in high-dimensional regimes, where noise accumulation can adversely affect linear discriminants \citep{fan2008highdim}. These factors provide a plausible explanation for the observed behavior on the HAR dataset. Notice, however, that LdaBoost outperforms GBM on the test set for the HAR dataset.
The paper has also limitations. 
Firstly, the simulation study is based on a multivariate normal data-generating process with class-specific means, a setting in which Linear Discriminant Analysis is known to be asymptotically optimal. As a consequence, the proposed LdaBoost method is evaluated in a scenario that is particularly favorable to its underlying structure, and the observed performance may therefore be partly expected. While this choice allows for a clear and interpretable comparison with the Bayes optimal classifier in a controlled framework, it does not fully reflect the complexity of real-world data, which often exhibit non-Gaussian distributions, nonlinear class boundaries, and heterogeneous feature types. Future work will extend the empirical analysis to more general settings, including non-Gaussian and nonlinear scenarios, in order to better assess the robustness and generalizability 
of the proposed approach.
Second, since LDA is traditionally defined for fixed class labels, the use of LdaBoost—where samples are relabeled based on residuals and LDA is iteratively refitted—may appear as a heuristic attempt to extend LDA to a setting that is more naturally suited to continuous targets, as in standard 
GBM. In the Discussion section, we provide a theoretical justification for this procedure, grounded in the gradient boosting framework of loss function minimization, as well as in related machine learning approaches that rely on pseudo-labels derived from residuals.
